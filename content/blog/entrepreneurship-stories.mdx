## Entrepreneurship as a Path to Independence

Self-employment is a powerful path to economic independence for PWDs who face employment discrimination. JOSSUD's entrepreneurship program has supported over 150 PWDs to start sustainable businesses.

## Meet Our Entrepreneurs

### Judith's Bakery Business

**Profile:** Judith, 32, Physical Disability (Uses Crutches)

Judith always loved baking but thought her disability would prevent her from running a business. Through JOSSUD's entrepreneurship training, she learned:

- Business planning and budgeting
- Marketing and customer relations
- Financial management
- Production planning

With a startup loan from JOSSUD, Judith opened a small bakery near her home. Today:

- **Monthly Income:** UGX 3 million+
- **Employees:** 2 other PWDs
- **Impact:** Her bakery is a gathering place for the community
- **Dream:** Expand to a full restaurant

*"JOSSUD didn't just give me money—they gave me knowledge and confidence. Now I mentor other PWDs interested in starting businesses."*

### David's Phone Repair Shop

**Profile:** David, 28, Hearing Impairment

David's hearing disability made him feel unemployable in the traditional job market. He had a passion for technology but no formal training.

JOSSUD:
- Provided vocational training in phone repair
- Connected him with mentors
- Facilitated access to startup capital
- Linked him with suppliers

Today, David's shop:

- **Location:** Busy market in Kampala
- **Monthly Revenue:** UGX 5 million+
- **Employees:** 3 staff including 2 PWDs
- **Recognition:** Known for quality repairs and fair prices
- **Expansion:** Planning to add a second shop

*"I used to see myself as a burden. Now I'm a business owner providing jobs. That's powerful."*

### Margaret's Fashion Design

**Profile:** Margaret, 35, Visual Impairment

Despite being blind, Margaret had a keen sense of fashion and design. Her family discouraged business dreams, believing her disability was a limitation.

JOSSUD's program:

- Provided business skills training tailored to her needs
- Connected her with assistants and business partners
- Facilitated connections with customers
- Supported business growth

Today, Margaret's business:

- **Specialization:** Custom clothing and fashion design
- **Clients:** Private clients and institutions
- **Monthly Income:** UGX 2.5 million+
- **Impact:** Employs 1 sighted assistant, inspiring women to pursue business
- **Achievement:** Won "PWD Entrepreneur of the Year" award

*"People used to pity me. Now they come to me for fashion advice. That shift in how people see me—that's the real business success."*

## The Entrepreneurship Model

JOSSUD's entrepreneurship support includes:

### Phase 1: Discovery & Training (3 months)
- Business skills courses
- Identifying viable business ideas
- Market research and opportunity analysis

### Phase 2: Planning & Startup (3 months)
- Detailed business planning
- Financial projections
- Access to startup capital (loans/grants)
- Supplier connections

### Phase 3: Launch & Growth (6+ months)
- Business coaching and mentoring
- Marketing support
- Accounting and financial management training
- Connection to market opportunities

### Phase 4: Sustainability & Scaling (Ongoing)
- Advanced business training
- Leadership development
- Networking with other entrepreneurs
- Support for expansion

## Why PWD Businesses Succeed

Our entrepreneurs attribute success to:

1. **Comprehensive Training** - Business skills go beyond just finance
2. **Mentorship** - Working with experienced business people
3. **Community Support** - VSLA groups that provide both capital and encouragement
4. **Accessibility Focus** - Understanding PWD perspectives on business design
5. **Peer Learning** - Learning from other PWD entrepreneurs
